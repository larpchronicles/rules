# Equipment

Equipment in Chronicles exists on two layers at once. There's the gear you bring as a player: the foam weapons, shields, packets, and costume pieces you physically carry onto the site, called [Phys Reps]. And there's the gear your character owns: the steel swords, enchanted rings, potions, and raw materials that exist in the world of your character, represented by [Item Tags]. This chapter covers both, and how the two fit together.

Phys Reps are about safety, play, and showing what's there to be interacted with in the game world. Combat reps in particular must be built to spec, so they're safe to use and fair in play.

Item Tags carry the other half: what an item is, what it does, and the bookkeeping that comes with it.

Most of what your character carries involves both at once: a padded sword in your hand, paired with a card that tells the game what that sword is.

## Phys Reps
**Physical Representations**, or Phys Reps (pronounced "fizz-reps") are the real world objects players use to represent the in-game items their characters posses and use.

### What is a Phys Rep?
A phys rep is the physical stand-in for something your character owns or uses. It is the real object you, the player, can actually pick up, swing, wear, or hand to someone else. Chronicles is a game you play with your body, not just your imagination: When your character attacks with a sword, you swing a padded boffer. When they cast a spell, you throw a packet. The phys rep is what lets everyone else see and physically interact with what your character is doing.

Phys reps do not need to be perfect replicas, but how they look defines your character's reality. Color, shape, markings — whatever the rep conveys is what your character sees. A black-and-green foam boffer is a black-and-green steel sword; a chainmail-patterned cloth shirt is a steel chain hauberk. If you want your character to carry a vine-covered shield, your shield rep needs to read as covered in vines; if your paladin character wouldn't wield a skull-headed mace, then your mace rep better not have a foam skull for a head.

What the world upgrades is the _substance_. To you, the boffer is a painted, foam-padded stick; to your character, it's a sharp long sword. That patterned shirt is cotton in your hands and riveted steel on your character's back. The rep settles what an item is and what it looks like; the item tag supplies its material properties and mechanical effects. If the rep were _literally_ what your character saw, then your yoga mat should be able to cut a steak.

Some imagination is always required, and the better a rep looks, the easier it comes. What matters is that the rep reads clearly as the thing it represents and that it's safe to use. Immersion is the goal, but the line gets drawn right where "immersive" would become "dangerous."

For the materials from which a phys rep may be made, or other construction details, check the individual phys rep construction sections below.

#### Shields
A shield phys rep must satisfy all of the following conditions to be legal:
- No straight-line measurement across the shield may exceed 36".
- Surface area ≤ 531 sq in. The actual covering surface (as if you taped over the entire face) must be under 531 sq in. Curved, domed, or cylindrical shields are measured along their curvature.
- The depth of a curved shield may be no greater than 6". When placed flat on a table, no part of the shield may extend greater than 6" from the surface of the table.
- A 12"-diameter circle must fit inside the shield's silhouetted\* area. Place a 12"-diameter circle on a table, then place the shield flat on top of the circle. The shield must completely cover the circle as viewed from directly above.
- Any protrusions extending in the plane of the face of the shield must not exceed 6" in length.
- Everything physically attached to the shield counts as part of the shield for all measurements above.

_(\* The shield's silhouette is defined as though a string were wrapped around the outer edge of the shield and pulled taut. Any gaps or voids within that silhouette still count as being part of the shield.)_

In essence, the shield must look and function like a shield. The Weapons Marshal at the event may fail a shield that technically meets these requirements but fails them in spirit. If you have any concern that your shield may not pass, please contact your local staff, they will be more than happy to help you construct or modify your shield to meet the requirements.

#### Common Weapon Failures
##### Pipe Foam Weapons
**Protruding Core Tip**
{{INSERT PICTURE/DIAGRAM OF LOCATION TO PRESS}}
At the tip of the sword, where the pipe foam meets the open cell thrusting tip, the core can often protrude past the pipe foam. Press your thumb between the pipe foam and open cell foam, you should not be able to feel the end of the core.

**Pipe Foam Seam**
{{INSERT PICTURE OF PIPE FOAM SEAM CHECK}}
Along the seam of the pipe foam, lengthwise along the blade: The adhesive that keeps the pipe foam closed will break down over time. Press your thumb along that seam, if the adhesive is breaking down, you'll feel the foam separate and be able to feel the core underneath.

**Tape Slipping**
All along the weapon, but especially at the open cell foam tip: The adhesive used on kite tape is not durable, especially through repeated heating/cooling cycles _(hot cars and frozen garages are murder on tape adhesives)._ The tape may unstick and/or slide around, exposing the foam and/or compromising the integrity of the weapon.
Solution:  Build in mechanical anchors instead of relying solely on adhesion. Run the tape in vertical strips along the blade, then wrap one strip horizontally all the way around the seam at the tip where the pipe foam meets the open cell thrusting tip. That encircling wrap prevents the lateral motion of the thrusting tip from peeling off the tape along the blade. Doing so leverages the adhesive's strongest mode (shear strength) rather than its weakest (peel strength). It also prevents the failure of a single piece of tape from cascading.

##### Shields
Bolts and other fasteners can rip out through foam board insulation. Spread the force of the fasteners across a larger surface on the board by using large washers, or create your own plastic backing plates (cutting the lid of a storage bin to shape and size works well for this).

### Spell Packets
Packets (aka "Spell Packets") are small (6"x6") squares of cloth filled with bird seed and held closed with a rubber band, or sewn shut. The head of the packet (where the birdseed is) is slightly smaller than a ping pong ball, and the tails of the packet are less than 5" long.

## Item Tags
### What Are Item Tags?

An **item tag** is a printed card — standard business-card size — that represents a single item your character owns and records its game-relevant properties: what the item is, what it does, and any [[durability]] or [[expiration]] it carries. If your character has it and it matters to the rules, it's on a tag.

### Phys Reps vs Item Tags

A **Phys Rep** is the real-world object you, the player, are holding. An **Item Tag** is the card that records what your _character_ is carrying and what it does. The rep is the prop; the tag is the item.

The two answer different questions. The rep answers _"What is the player physically holding?"_ — a foam blade, a packet, a board wrapped in duct tape. The tag answers _"What does the character have, and what are its game properties?"_ — a masterwork steel longsword, a vial of healing draught, three ingots of star-iron.

> [!EXAMPLE] You walk into the tavern carrying a foam sword and a card reading _"Longsword."_ Out of game, that's a length of pool noodle and a slip of cardstock. In game, your character wears a finely balanced steel longsword at their hip.

### Which Items Need a Tag

Every item that has a game-mechanical effect requires a tag: weapons, shields, armor, potions, scrolls, crafting materials, magic items, and the like. The tag is what makes the item _real_ in the game world. No tag, no item.

Items with no mechanical effect do not need tags. Clothing, boots, rope, lanterns, a tankard, a deck of cards, and similar props are just set dressing for your character. Carry as many as you like.

> [!NOTE] Coin is the exception that isn't really an exception: it has no tag because it _is_ its own phys rep. Chronicles uses custom-minted brass and nickel tokens as physical currency, so the coin in your pouch represents itself.

### Which Items Need a Phys Rep

An item needs a phys rep when other players physically interact with it during play: anything swung in combat, blocked with, worn, or brandished. **Weapons, shields, armor, and worn or wielded magic items all require a phys rep**, because the physical world has to be able to touch them — you can't block a blow with a sword that doesn't exist.

For potions, scrolls, and crafting materials, the tag _is_ the rep. There's nothing to swing or wear, so the card itself fully represents the item.

### Standard Reps and Unique Reps

Most items are generic. One longsword is much like another, so a standard tag just names the item type, and you may represent it with any legal rep you like. If someone hands you a longsword tag, use your own blade, swap reps, whatever's convenient.

Magic items and other unique items have a _specific_ recognizable rep, and the tag tells you so — either by describing the rep, by giving the item a unique name, or both.

> [!EXAMPLE] A standard tag reads simply: **Longsword**
> 
> The Sword of Yremmel reads: **Vine-covered Sword** _The Sword of Yremmel_

When a tag specifies its rep, you must use that rep. You can't carry the Sword of Yremmel as a plain foam blade; it's the vine-covered one or nothing. This is also why lending matters: lend a standard longsword and the borrower can use any rep, but lend the Sword of Yremmel and they take the specific rep along with it. If a unique rep is damaged, lost, or otherwise needs to change, your local plot team can make accommodations.

### Pairing Reps and Tags

For any item that needs both, a usable item is a rep and its matching tag, together with you. You keep the tag on your person — typically in a pouch — rather than fastened to the rep.

Because the tag belongs with its rep, **the rep is the thing that changes hands in play.** If your sword is disarmed, dropped, lent, or taken, it's the rep that physically leaves your control, and whoever ends up holding the rep is entitled to its tag. (The mechanics of taking an item from another character are covered under [[theft]].)

> [!EXAMPLE] Mid-fight, Alice lends Bob her enchanted sword. She hands him the rep and he can use it immediately — combat doesn't pause for paperwork. At the next natural break, she passes him the matching tag. She shouldn't hold up the fight to do it, but she shouldn't wander off with the tag, either.

You may **have a rep with no tag** — but you can't use it as an item. As far as the game is concerned, your character is empty-handed.

You may **have a tag with no rep** — but you can't use that item either, and it's your job to fix that. Acquire a valid rep at your earliest convenience, or stow the surplus tags in your cabin. Until you do, the item is inert in play.

You may **not** carry more usable copies of an item than you have reps for. One shield rep and five shield tags would mean your character is hauling four spare shields around in their pockets.[^belt]

[^belt]: You're going to need a much better belt.

> [!WARNING] Carrying surplus tags can never help you and can still hurt you. A tag with no matching rep can't be used — but it can still be stolen, destroyed, or otherwise lost. Holding extra tags is all downside.

### Reps Belong to Their Owners

A phys rep is a real object that a real person bought or built. It always belongs to the player who owns it, no matter where it ends up in play. If you come into possession of another player's rep — through in-game theft, a dropped weapon, a loan, or anything else — you're responsible for returning it to its owner, either directly or through a marshal.

> [!WARNING] Your _character_ may steal from other characters; that's part of the game. _You_ may never steal from another player. Keeping someone's rep, walking off with their property out of game — that's real theft, and it's treated as a serious code-of-conduct violation.
