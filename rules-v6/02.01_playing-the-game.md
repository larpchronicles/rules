# Playing the Game

## In Game vs. Out of Game

At a Chronicles event, everything exists in one of two states: **In Game (IG)** or **Out of Game (OOG)**. This applies to locations, players, and items.

When something is In Game, it exists in the world of Evren. You and other characters can see it, interact with it, and be affected by it. When something is Out of Game, it does not exist in the game world. Your character cannot perceive or interact with it, and it cannot affect your character.

### Players: Going In and Out of Game

A player who is In Game is actively portraying their character. A player who is Out of Game is just themselves, their character isn't present. 

To go Out of Game, you should move to a discreet or out-of-the-way location, ask out loud "Does anyone mind if I go Out of Game?", then quietly do a 10-count ("One I walk into the woods, Two I walk into the woods..." etc.) and put on a **white headband** or place your hand (or a held item) on top of your head.

While Out of Game, you are invisible to the game world. No character may interact with you, and you may not interact with any character or game element. You should move out of the way of any active game play if possible, and avoid lingering in areas where your presence might disrupt immersion.

To go In Game requires a 3-count, as appropriate for the situation — "One I come from the forest, Two I come from the forest, Three I come from the forest," or a skeleton might use "One I rise from the ground..." Remove your white headband, proceed with the count, and once completed, you are In Game.

A player may go Out of Game at any time for any reason. No explanation is required for this, though they are expected to move away so as not to disrupt any players remaining In Game.

#### After Player-vs-Player Conflict

After initiating hostile actions against another player's character, such as combat, theft, or other acts of aggression, a player may not go Out of Game for 30 minutes. This ensures that the affected player has the opportunity to respond in game. This rule is enforced in the spirit of fair play. A player who genuinely needs to go Out of Game for personal reasons will not be penalized for doing so, but a pattern of using OOG transitions to avoid the consequences of in-game actions will be addressed by staff.

### Locations: IG and OOG Areas

Nearly the entire site is considered In Game. Your cabin, the tavern, the paths between them, and the surrounding woods are all part of the game world. 

The following areas are always Out of Game:
- **Logistics / NPC Camp**
- **Bathrooms and showers**
- **Parking lots**

Players may not chase or follow another character into an Out of Game area, and may not use an Out of Game areas to hide from In Game events.

Some areas are In Game but designated as **No-Combat zones**. In these areas, your character is present and can interact with others, but combat is not permitted. If combat is imminent, players should call a Hold! and relocate everyone involved to an acceptable In-Game area. Kitchens and other areas with safety concerns are common No-Combat zones. Staff will identify these areas during PC Talk.

### Items: In Game and Out of Game

In Game items are anything with an item tag or that is clearly part of the game world (weapons, armor, coins, potions, etc.). Out of Game items are personal belongings (phones, car keys, wallet, sleeping bag, medications, etc.).

Keep these separate. Store OOG items under your bed or in a container marked "OOG." In Game items should never be stored in a way that makes them inaccessible to the game world (such as locked in your car or hidden in an OOG container). Items that are brought into game cannot later be stored in an OOG area, and any items kept in an OOG area cannot be brought In Game. 

### Headbands
#### Orange Headbands - Non Combatants & Pages
Pages are people that are unable to enter combat. They may be children or adults, although any child under age 14 must be a Page. Pages may not use combat skills, weapons, or throw packets.

**You may not ever physically strike a Page.** If you engage in combat with a page, you may point your weapon or packet and say, "Page, I kill you." They cannot participate in combat. They are treated as if they have 1 body.

#### White Headbands - Out of Game

Once you arrive on site, you are entering an In Game Area. If you are not in game as your character yet, you must have a white headband on. If you do not have a white headband, you may request one from staff. If there are none available, you may signify your out of game status by holding a hand to your forehead or touching the top of your head with a held item. This gesture is accepted as an out of character signal and frequently used by NPCs when not in game.

If you are in game, you should not react or speak to anyone that is wearing a white headband without first going out of game yourself. It is generally seen as poor etiquette to break the environment for others.

When your character dies and your dying count has expired, after dropping all in game items, you should put on a white headband and go to Logistics/NPC Camp to register your death. Your spirit cannot interact in any manner with others.

## Using Skills, Abilities, and Spells
A character's skills, abilities, and spells are the mechanical tools used to interact with the game world. Skills are generally always available, and are acquired using Skill Points. Abilities and spells cost resources (Stamina Points, Agility Points, or Mana Points, depending on the ability) to utilize. Such resources can be recovered by Resting.

### Battleboards: Tracking your skills
When you check in, you'll receive a **battleboard** along with your character sheet. Your battleboard is a tracking sheet that lists your character's abilities, spells, and resource pools. As you use abilities and spend resources during the event, you'll mark them off on the battleboard. This is how both you and staff keep track of what you've used and what you have remaining. 

### Resting
Resting is how your character recovers spent resources and refreshes their abilities. After a Rest, your Mana Points, Stamina Points, and Agility Points return to full, and any per-Rest abilities are renewed.

To Rest, your character must be in the **In Town area** (the tavern and PC cabins). Head to Logistics, have your current battleboard signed off by a staff member, and pick up a fresh one. The time spent walking to Logistics and handling the swap counts toward the 10-minute rest period.

During a Rest, your character is recuperating by stretching, meditating, catching their breath, sharpening a blade, or whatever fits their personality. You may not use game skills, engage in combat, or take any other strenuous in-game action while resting. If any of these things interrupt your rest, you must start over.

During an event, a character may Rest a limited number of times: **(2 × event days) − 1**. For a typical 2-day event, this means 3 Rests. For a 3-day event, 5 Rests. Spend them wisely.

> [!NOTE] NPC Shifts and Resting 
> A player may choose to volunteer for an NPC shift during their rest time, earning additional rewards while their character recuperates. See the NPC section for details.

### Per-Day Abilities and Effects

Some abilities, effects, and magic items do not recharge through Resting. Instead, they recharge once per day at **6:00 PM** (out of game). This happens automatically and does not count as a Rest.

Production skills, certain magic items, and any ability or effect labeled "Per Day" or "Daily" follow this schedule.


## Body and Armor
Your character's survivability is measured by two values: **Body** and **Armor**.

### Body

Body represents your character's physical health. All characters start with **10 Body** and can gain more through the Toughness skill.

**Current Body** is the amount of Body your character has right now. **Maximum Body** is the highest value to which your character can be healed. **Temporary Body** is Body that exists in excess of your Maximum Body, granted by certain effects. Temporary Body is always lost before regular Body, and once lost, it cannot be healed or recovered.

When you take damage, it reduces your Current Body. Regardless of how much damage you take, your Body can never drop below -1.

### Armor

Armor provides an additional layer of protection on top of Body. Damage is always deducted from Armor first, then from Body once Armor is depleted. 

There are two sources of Armor:

**Physical Armor** is actual armor that your character — and you! — wear. Physical armor must be represented by real armor or a convincing costume piece (a physrep) and is typically made from leather, chain, plate, or similar materials (See [[Armor Phys Reps]]). When you check in, your armor phys rep will be assessed and assigned an Armor Value. The Armor Value represents the highest value armor tag that this phys rep can represent.

**Dexterity Armor** represents your character's agility and ability to avoid or deflect blows. It costs more Skill Points for the same amount of armor as physical armor, but requires no phys rep as it is an innate quality of your character.

Physical Armor and Dexterity Armor do not stack. You may only benefit from one at a time. Upon refitting a suit of armor, all other sources of armor you may posses are reduced to zero.

#### Refitting Armor
**Refitting Armor** restores your armor to its full value. Refitting Physical Armor requires 1 minute of concentration, spent roleplaying adjusting straps, pushing out dents, tightening buckles. Refitting Dexterity Armor also requires 1 minute, roleplayed as stretching, shadowboxing, or similar physical conditioning. 

A character may not walk or use any skills during a refit. If the refit is interrupted (by taking damage, being affected by an ability, or any other game interaction), it must be restarted from the beginning.

## States of Health 
### How alive are you, really?

Your character's state depends on their Current Body:

#### Conscious
**Conscious** (1+ Body): Your character can act normally. You are able to move, speak, fight, cast spells, use items and abilities.

#### Unconscious
**Unconscious** (0 Body): Your character collapses and is unaware of the world around them. They cannot move, speak, or take any game actions. After 1 minute, the character wakes up with 1 Body. 

#### Bleeding Out
A character reduced below 0 Body drops to **-1 Body** (regardless of how much damage the final blow dealt) and begins **Bleeding Out**. The character is unconscious and dying.

Bleeding Out lasts **1 minute**. During this time:

- Any healing effect will restore the character's Body by the stated amount (calculated from -1) and end the Bleeding Out state. The character regains consciousness immediately.
- **First Aid** will pause the Bleeding Out count. First Aid takes 1 minute of uninterrupted concentration. Once complete, the character is at 0 Body and Unconscious (and will wake with 1 Body after 1 minute). If the person performing First Aid is interrupted, the Bleeding Out count resumes where it left off.

After 1 minute of Dying without healing or First Aid, the character is now Dead.

> [!EXAMPLE] 
> Ulysses the Unlucky is Dying. Thirty seconds in, his friend casts a Cure Light Wounds 10, which heals Ulysses for 10 Body. His Current Body goes from -1 to 9. He is Conscious and back in the fight.

#### Dead
While Dead:

- The character is not aware of their surroundings and cannot take any game actions.
- The character has **5 minutes** to receive a **Life** spell, **Revivify** spell, or other Life effect. If they do, they are restored to life as described by the spell or effect.
- Any temporary spell effects and status effects on the character end when they die, included prepared defenses and buffs. These are not regained if the character receives a Life effect, though new Defenses can be prepared and new buffs applied via the normal rules. Permanent effects and any effects that explicitly persists through death are retained.
 
After 5 minutes of being Dead, the character's body is no longer strong enough to hold onto the spirit. The player announces **"Body dissipates,"** and the character's spirit seeks resurrection.

### Dissipation and Resurrection

The player announces **"Body dissipates,"** drops all in-game items to the ground, puts on a white headband, and goes Out of Game. The player should then find a marshal or head to Logistics to register the death.

The character's spirit departs to seek resurrection at the nearest safe **Resurrection Circle**. Deciding to resurrect at a given Resurrection Circle is an Out of Game decision, a player may not be compelled to resurrect in a circle the player considers to be unsafe for their character.

A player may choose to refuse any mechanical effect by voluntarily dissipating instead of taking the effect. This is solely an Out of Game decision, and thus, a character can never be forced to dissipate in this manner. The character drops all in-game items and the player goes Out of Game to seek resurrection as normal.

> [!NOTE] Regarding Resurrection
> A player may always decline to resurrect at any specific Resurrection Circle. This decision is purely Out of Game on the part of the player, and does not require explanation or justification. Staff will identify alternate locations the character may choose to resurrect at.

When a character's spirit arrives at a Resurrection Circle, the character reforms with full Body, full Armor, and restored skills. All non-ritual, non-permanent effects are removed upon resurrection.

Resurrection incurs **SP Debt**, which is described in the Skills chapter.


### Killing Blow

A Killing Blow is a deliberate finishing strike against a helpless target. To perform a Killing Blow, place a weapon (or your hand) against the target's torso and count: **"Killing Blow 1, Killing Blow 2, Killing Blow 3"** followed by a damage type or spell incant. If the count is completed without interruption, the target becomes Dead.

A Killing Blow's damage type matches whatever the character would normally swing. A barehanded Killing Blow is Normal. A character may also use a spell incant in place of a damage type.

A Killing Blow can be interrupted by any attack that strikes the person performing it. The target of a Killing Blow may refuse it if they are capable of movement — a Killing Blow against a conscious, mobile target is assumed to be automatically refused. A Killing Blow can only succeed against a target that is incapacitated, restrained, or otherwise unable to move, or accepted willingly.

### Carrying a Body

A character that is Unconscious, Bleeding Out, or Dead may be carried by another character. To pick up a body, roleplay lifting them on a three count: **"One I pick you up, two I pick you up, three I pick you up."** Both players must remain within arm's reach of each other, and the carrying player may not move faster than a walk. If any effect forces the carrier to move quickly or prevents them from holding items, they must drop the body on a one count: **"One I drop you."**

## Defenses

Defenses allow your character to negate or avoid incoming attacks. Most defenses are granted by skills, abilities, spells, or items, and each one specifies what types of attacks it can defend against.

### Defense Slots

Every character has **1 Defense Slot** by default, with additional slots available through the Additional Defense Slot skill. Defense Slots limit how many defenses you can have ready at any given time.

A defense must be **prepared** in a Defense Slot before it can be used. Only **Active Defenses** — defenses that are currently prepared in a slot — may be used to defend against attacks. You may have as many Active Defenses as you have Defense Slots.

### Preparing Defenses

To prepare a defense, you speak the preparation verbal associated with the ability. This is typically a short phrase that identifies what you're preparing.

> [!EXAMPLE] Preparation Verbals
> 
> - **Parry**: "I prepare my defenses against Physical."
> - **Resist Magic**: "I prepare my defenses against Magic."
> - **Barrier Flame**: "I call forth mystic force to barrier Flame."

If all of your Defense Slots are full and you wish to prepare a new defense, you must replace one of your current Active Defenses. You choose which one to drop.

Some effects granted by items or plot-specific abilities function as Active Defenses but do not require a Defense Slot. These will always state so explicitly in their description.

### Using Defenses

When you are hit by an attack that one of your Active Defenses applies to, you may call **"Resist"** or **"Dodge"** (as appropriate to the defense) to negate the attack entirely. The defense is then expended and the Defense Slot it occupied is now empty.

If a single attack contains multiple components (such as a weapon blow with a carrier effect), and your defense applies to any part of it, the entire attack is negated.

Active Defenses that have been used can be re-prepared by speaking the preparation verbal again, provided you have the ability or effect available to do so.

All Active Defenses are lost if the character dies and is restored by a Life effect or Revivify. They must be re-prepared after returning to life.

## Combat
Combat at Chronicles is enacted by physically contacting another player with either a weapon physrep or a packet. 

### Communication is Your Greatest Weapon
Communication between players is critical for combat going smoothly. It is important to speak clearly and enunciate your calls, and it is equally important to have patience with someone that asks you to repeat your call because they were unable to hear or understand it.  

If, at any time, you are unsure of what just happened in combat, ask your combat partner to repeat their call for you, or ask the people near to repeat what the calls were. If you do not know what a call means or how it affects you, simply state "I don't know what that does" or "What does that do?" Either to your opponent, to someone nearby, or to the marshal of the encounter. 

If in doubt in any situation, speak with the marshal for the encounter or a member of staff and tell them the situation. They are there to help you out!

### Delivering Weapon Blows
Attacking somebody with a weapon is, all together, pretty intuitive for most people. Just in case, though, here's how it works:
#### Melee Weapons
Every time you would like to deal damage to another character with a melee weapon, you must:
- Announce your [[Damage Call]] before or as you swing your weapon. 
- Pull the weapon back and swing it through an arc of at least 45 degrees prior to making contact with the target. _(you cannot simply "wipe" someone to death!)_
- Lightly tap the target with the padded striking surface of the weapon (See [[Phys Rep Construction]]).

#### Ranged Weapons
To make an attack with a bow or crossbow, the weapon phys rep must be held out in front you, as if you were preparing to fire a real archery bow. With the other hand, you must touch a packet to the bow or crossbow, then pull your hand back towards your ear as if you were drawing the string of a bow. Once you have done this, you may make your [[Damage Call]], then throw the packet at your target. 

#### Thrown Weapons
Thrown weapon follow all the same rules as packet-delivered abilities. Additionally, they may never be used as a melee weapon or to block a melee attack.

#### Valid Boffer Strikes
In order for a weapon blow to succeed, the physrep must strike the target's body on a valid striking location. If the striking weapon physrep contacts a wielded weapon or shield *in any way* prior to contacting the target's body, the strike is considered to be blocked. "Powering through" a player's blocking weapon or shield is not a valid strike

The head, throat, spine, and groin region are ***never*** valid strike locations. Additionally, when wielding a weapon the character has the skill to use, that player's hands become invalid striking locations. Thus, if a player attempts to block a weapon blow with their own held weapon, and the blow strikes the hand wielding the weapon, the strike is considered to be successfully blocked.

Any player that repeatedly, flagrantly, or intentionally violates the rules of safe combat will face disciplinary action.

#### Speed of Combat
Every attack with a weapon must be accompanied by a discrete damage call. A player may not attack faster than they are able to make their damage calls, or once per second, whichever is slower. _(Accommodations may be made for anyone who may struggle with damage calls because of a physical limitation, speech impediment, or any other concern by speaking with the staff at an event.)_

#### Light Touch Combat
When striking with a weapon, light contact is all that is required to land a successful attack. Strikes should feel like playing a game of tag, not a game of football. Swinging for the fences is unnecessary and disallowed, the focus should be on "tapping" the target.

### Safety
**The safety of players at Chronicles is paramount.** 
Chronicles enforces a Light Touch boffer combat system. While Chronicles is explicitly inclusive of players with widely varying physical capabilities, and the combat is intended to be accessible to everyone, it is still an inherently physical activity. 

#### Non-Combatant Players
Players that would like to abstain from boffer combat may indicate their "No Combat" status by wearing an orange headband, and must be avoided in boffer combat. No Combat players are still affected by game effects, and their characters may be killed by announcing "I slay you" while within melee striking range of the target.

#### Hold!
The word "Hold" as a very specific use at Chronicles: **It immediately and unquestioningly halts play**.

It is important that you do not move your feet when this happens. Anyone can call a hold. Players, unless they are no longer able to call hold, should only call holds for themselves. When they are fit to do so, they will be the one to resume play by calling "Lay On!" as described above.

When this happens you need to pay attention to what is going on around you. Stop all activity, movement, and be silent unless directed otherwise. Do not move, "step out of the way", or kneel down, because you never know when a hold was called because someone's glasses are in danger of being crushed.

Holds are used for a number of reasons: 
- Combat has become unsafe in some way
- A player is injured or has fallen in danger
- Staff needs to give a description to the players

 Avoid in-character/game interactions where you might use the word "hold", especially loudly. Desist, stop, and halt all convey similar meanings and won't cause an unintentional hold. 

#### Charging (aka the arms' length rule)
At no point during combat are players allowed to come within arms' reach of each other. All players involved in a combat are responsible for maintaining this distance. If a player can extend their arm and touch their opponent, then those players must halt their combat and separate, safely, before continuing play. If it is not possible for the two players to create enough distance from each other due to terrain or other considerations, then they must call a Hold to resolve the situation, such as by relocating to an area with enough space to proceed safely.

#### Turtling
Turtling is the act of occluding all valid strike zones, while in melee combat, from an opponent through the use of a shield or the combined use of a shield and other objects. This includes hunkering down completely behind a shield, or standing across a table from an opponent and shielding your top half. An opponent must always have at least one option to strike you without engaging in unsafe behavior. 

#### Blind Striking
In order to attack a target with a boffer, the player must be able to see their target. "Blind striking" around corners, doorways, or other obstructions through which the target cannot be seen is explicitly disallowed.

#### Trapping
A player may never use any device that entraps or immobilizes an opponent's physrep. This includes pinning an opponent's weapon against the ground or other object, catching an opponents by hand, entrapping an opponent's weapon using fabric or rope, closing an opponent's weapon in a door,  or using devices such as a catching dagger.

### Packet-Delivered Abilities
Casting a spell, firing a ranged weapon, and using packet-delivered abilities all require hitting a target with a [[Packet]].

To cast a spell or use a packet-delivered ability, the player must:
- Hold a packet in a free hand
- Speak the spell incant or ability verbal
- Throw the packet at the intended target

#### Disrupted Spells
If the player's spell incant or throw is disrupted, the resources used to cast the spell or ability are lost as though it were successfully cast. Taking damage does not necessarily interrupt casting, however, any effect or state that would prevent spellcasting (such as Stun, Silence, or dying) interrupts the casting of a spell.

#### Touch Casting
Touch casting involves holding a packet, and, while within arm's length of another player, speaking the incant or verbal and touching the player on the shoulder with the packet. It is entirely acceptable for a player to decline touch contact, in which case, the caster should still be within arm's reach of the target and simply gesture as if they were going to touch the target's shoulder with the packet.

The intention of touch casting is to facilitate the casting of spells that you would be reasonably certain your target would perceive as beneficial, without having to throw and retrieve a spell packet. The target of a touch cast may refuse the effect by stating "Refused" after the caster casts the spell. It is as if the caster hit the target with a packet, but the target was unaffected by the spell; The resource cost of the spell or ability is still expended and may not be recovered via [[Meditate]].

To refuse a touch cast, the target must be conscious and capable of moving their arms and/or legs. In other words, refusing a touch cast is basically your character moving or slapping the caster's hand away. In a situation where the target is capable of refusing the touch cast, and fails to acknowledge to the caster that the spell was taken, it can be assumed that the target refused the touch cast.

Touch casting may **never** be used with hostile intent during combat against a target that is not otherwise incapacitated. Doing so is unsafe, and would violate the [[Charging]] rules. Example situations where touch casting is disallowed:
- Casting a spell on an enemy you or your allies are currently fighting against
- Attempting to cast a Rift Lock or other spell on a target currently Rifting in or out
- Casting a spell on a target that is dragging your friend away

### Spell Strikes
Spell Strike, Physical Strike, Toxin Strike, and Aether Strike are all spells or spell-like abilities delivered with melee phys reps. Instead of using the weapon for a standard weapon blow, the character announces the ability verbal then swings with their weapon phys rep. Striked abilities differ from Weapon Blows in a number of ways:
- The weapon strike does not need to contact the target's body. Like spell packets, the strike need only contact the target's body or a possession of the target (ex: hitting your target's cloak without contacting their body is a valid strike).
- The ability is consumed immediately, and is only good for a single swing; if the player misses their target entirely, the ability is still expended.
- Striked abilities are not blocked by shields or weapons. A strike that contacts the targets shield or weapons is a successful hit.

### Blocking Weapons and Abilities
Weapon blows may be blocked with a wielded shield or weapon, so long as the character has the skill to use to it when they attempt to block. Blocking without having the relevant skill or while skilless (ie, while [[Stunned]]) results in the player taking the hit as though they had not blocked it at all.

Spells and abilities (anything delivered as Physical, Magic, Toxin, Aether, and their Strike equivalents) successfully lands on the target if the packet or weapon phys rep makes contact with any part of the target's body, clothing, held items, or possessions.

## Verbals
To affect another character mechanically, you must use a **Verbal**. A Verbal tells other players what game mechanic you are using and how it should be resolved. A verbal can take one of four forms: Ability Verbals, Incants, Damage Calls, and Defenses. 

Most effects in play follow this process:
1. Use a Skill, Ability, Spell, item, or other rule that allows you to act.
2. Deliver it using the appropriate **delivery method**.
3. Speak the appropriate **Verbal**.
4. If the delivery is successful, the target resolves the damage or effect.
5. The target may use a **Defense**, if they have one that applies.

#### Types of Verbals
There are four main kinds of Verbals:
- **Damage Calls** are used to deliver weapon damage. They always begin with a number, such as `5 Normal` or `3 Magic Flame`.
- **Ability Verbals** are used to deliver Effects. They take the form `<Type> <Effect>`, such as `Physical Stun` or `Magic Sleep`.
- **Incants** are used to cast Spells. An Incant functions as the Spell’s verbal delivery.
- **Defenses** are used in response to negate or avoid an incoming Effect or attack.

### Damage Calls
Directly dealing damage requires a weapon blow. There are three components to a damage call: 
- Damage Amount
- Damage Type
- Carrier (optional)

> [!EXAMPLE] Examples of damage calls:
> 5 Normal
> 3 Magic Flame
> 10 Poison Sleep

#### Damage Amount
Generally, this amount is calculated based on the number of relevant Weapon Proficiencies and Backstabs the character has (though it can be modified by some skills and abilities). It is the amount of damage that will be inflicted to target should the attack be successful. The Damage Amount may never be less than 0.  

#### Damage Type
There are 5 Damage Types:
- Normal
- Silver
- Poison
- Magic
- Aether

Some creatures may be immune or resistant to certain damage types _(such as werewolves, which are immune to Normal, Poison, and Magic, thus requiring a Silvered weapon to deal damage to them)_.

Aether is an exceptionally rare damage type. Active defenses cannot be used to defend against Weapon blows delivered with the "Aether" damage type _(ie, they cannot be Resisted or Dodged)_. They can be blocked with a weapon or shield like any other weapon blow, however.

By default, all weapons use the Normal damage type. However, _Aura_ effects may grant characters or specific items the ability to utilize other damage types. 

Items that have an aura property _must_ swing for that damage type, unless superseded by another explicitly-stated effect _(such as a character with an Aura ability)_.

#### Carriers
Carriers impart the named effect to the target if the weapon blow successfully reduces the target's current body points. It functions the same as if the target were affected by a Strike ability of the appropriate type:

| Damage Type    | Strike Delivery |
| -------------- | --------------- |
| Normal, Silver | Physical Strike |
| Magic          | Spell Strike    |
| Poison         | Toxin Strike    |
| Aether         | Aether Strike   |
> [!EXAMPLE] Example
Ebenezer the Elf is fighting some giant spiders when one of them hits him with 5 Poison Sleep. Ebenezer has 20 Body, but no armor, so when the hit connects, Ebenezer the Elf will lose 5 Body and fall asleep as though they were hit by a Toxin Strike Sleep.

Unless explicitly stated, a character is always able to drop their carrier from their weapon blows. Only one carrier may be used per weapon blow, so even if the character has many carrier abilities, they may only use at most 1 per swing.
> [!EXAMPLE]
Leonardo the Lich has the transform abilities Carrier: Drain and Carrier: Stun. Bob can choose to swing 5 Magic, OR 5 Magic Drain, OR 5 Magic Stun. Leonardo **cannot** swing 5 Magic Drain Stun.

Note: Abilities and effects that cause damage or healing, such as Mana Bolt and Heal Light Wounds, can never function as Damage Carriers.

#### Damage Call Examples
> [!EXAMPLE]
> Anduvius the Adventurer has a normal longsword, a transform with the Magic Aura and Flame Aura abilities, and a Frost coating applied to the weapon. Anduvius can choose to deliver any of the following weapon blows:
> * 5 Magic
> * 5 Normal
> * 5 Magic Flame
> * 5 Normal Flame
> * 5 Magic Frost
> * 5 Normal Frost

> [!EXAMPLE]
> Velma the Vampire Hunter has a silvered long sword. They may only swing 5 Silver with it. If they wish to swing 5 Normal, they must use a different weapon.

> [!EXAMPLE]
> Quentin the Quicksilver Construct has the transform ability Silver Aura. While wielding a normal Long Sword, Quentin may choose to swing 5 Normal OR 5 Silver, at their discretion.

> [!EXAMPLE]
> Miracle Max has the ability Poison Aura and a long sword enchanted with Magic Aura. Max may only choose to swing 5 Poison OR 5 Magic with that sword.
  
> [!EXAMPLE]
> Archibald the Artificer has a silvered short sword. He decides to use his artificing skill to enchant the sword with a Magic Aura. The resulting weapon is no longer considered silvered, and will only have Magic Aura on the item tag.

### Ability Verbals
Ability verbals have two components: Delivery Type, and Effect. They will always take the form of `<Delivery Type> <Effect>`.
#### Delivery Methods
A **delivery method** is the real-world way the player delivers a mechanic. This is the physrep or medium used at the event.

Delivery Methods are:
- Weapon Physrep
- Packet
- Potion Physrep
- Voice Radius

#### Delivery Types
A **delivery type** is the in-world nature of an Ability or Effect. It describes what the character is using fictionally, not what the player is holding physically.

Delivery Types can be:

### Incants
Incants are verbals used to cast spells. They are as if the ability were delivered as `Magic <Spell Name>`, except that the character must be capable of speaking to cast the spell.

### Botched and Incorrect Incants and Verbals
If a player accidentally or mistakenly fails to correctly speak a verbal or incant, including interruption for any reason, the spell or ability automatically fails. The results are the same as if the caster were disrupted while casting the spell (See [[Disrupted Spells]]).
